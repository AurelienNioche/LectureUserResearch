1. Define a research problem & the hypothesis¶

Course Overview

The research problem constitutes the question(s) you aim to answer

The hypothesis constitute(s) the answer(s) to this(these) possible question(s)

 Important


Strong restriction: the hypothesis has to be a falsifiable statement (Popper, 1959 as a response to the problem of induction of Hume, 1739-40)

For a contextualized explanation, you can refer to the Stanford Encyclopedia: Andersen, Hanne and Brian Hepburn, "Scientific Method", The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Summer 2016 Edition), Edward N. Zalta (ed.), URL = https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/sum2016/entries/scientific-method/.

H: "All swans are white"

H: "Most people do not really want freedom, because freedom involves responsibility, and most people are frightened of responsibility."

Is this statement falsifiable?

Examples of non falsifiable statements:

  • tautologies
  • vague open-ended statements

Note: do not cofound *working* hypothesis and *testing* hypothesis

 Important


Be aware of the confirmation bias

...but even using a scientific method, we can be tempted...

Course Overview

 Important

  • between subjects design
  • within subjects design

5.2 Compute descriptive statistics¶

Course Overview

...and only thereafter summarize the information, computing descriptive statistics: mean, median, standard deviation or standard error, ...

 Important


Differenciate mean and median
\begin{equation} \mathrm {median} (x)={\frac {1}{2}}(x_{\lfloor (n+1)/2\rfloor }+x_{\lceil (n+1)/2\rceil }) \end{equation}

where $x$ is an ordered list of $n$ numbers, and $ \lfloor \cdot \rfloor $ and $\lceil \cdot \rceil $ denote the floor and ceiling functions.

The floor function is the function that takes as input a real number $i$, and gives as output the greatest integer less than or equal to $i$. Similarly, the ceiling function maps $i$ to the least integer greater than or equal to $i$.

Example ($n$ is odd): 1, 3, 3, 6, 7, 8, 9

Example ($n$ is even): 1, 3, 6, 7, 8, 9

\begin{equation}\mathrm{mean}(x) ={\frac {1}{n}}\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}{x_{i}}\right)={\frac {x_{1}+x_{2}+\cdots +x_{n}}{n}} \end{equation}

where x is an ordered list of n numbers

Why is it important?


Let's take an example!

Let's considerate data $D_N$ that follows a normal distribution, that is such that:

$$ D_N\sim \mathcal{N} (\mu ,\sigma )$$$$ f(x)={\frac {1}{\sigma {\sqrt {2\pi }}}}e^{-{\frac {1}{2}}\left({\frac {x-\mu }{\sigma }}\right)^{2}}$$

Let's now consider data $D_G$ that follows a Gamma distribution, that is such that: $$ D_G \sim \mathrm{Gamma} (\alpha ,\beta )$$ the probability density function of the Gamma distribution being : $${\begin{aligned}f(x;\alpha ,\beta )&={\frac {\beta ^{\alpha }x^{\alpha -1}e^{-\beta x}}{\Gamma (\alpha )}}\quad {\text{ for }}x>0\quad \alpha ,\beta >0,\\[6pt]\end{aligned}}$$ where $\Gamma (\alpha )$ is the gamma function. For all positive integers, $ \Gamma (\alpha )=(\alpha -1)!$

5.4 Discuss the implications of the results¶

Course Overview

 Important


Differentiate internal and external validity
  • Internal: relative to your data; are the manipulation that you did the (main) cause of what you observe?
  • External: relative to the relation between your data and the outside world; can we generalize what you observe?
